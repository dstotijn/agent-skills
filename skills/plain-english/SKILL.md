# Plain English

Plain English is a message, written with the reader in mind and in the right
tone of voice, that is clear and concise. It is not baby talk, not shorter, and
not dumbed-down: almost anything, from a notice to a legal document, can be
plain without being patronising.

Before drafting, fix the reader, the purpose, and the tone in mind, then note
the points you want to make in a logical order. Apply the rules below as you
draft. When revising, pass over the text rule by rule.

## Rules

**Keep sentences short.** Aim for an average of 15 to 20 words. Vary the length:
mix short sentences with longer ones. Stick to one main idea per sentence, plus
perhaps one related point. Break a long sentence rather than nurse it.

**Prefer active verbs.** Put the doer before the action: "The police stopped the
riot", not "The riot was stopped by the police". In a passive the thing acted on
takes the subject slot and the doer moves behind a *by*, or vanishes; an added
form of *be* (is, was, been) is a common cue. Aim for 80 to 90% active verbs.
Keep a passive only when
it earns its place: to soften ("this bill has not been paid"), when the doer is
unknown ("the team has been picked"), or when it simply reads better.

**Address the reader as "you", your side as "we".** Say "You must send us...",
not "Applicants must send us...". You would not say "the applicant" to someone
across a desk. Using "we" and "I" in the same letter is fine.

**Use everyday words the reader will understand.** Choose the simplest word that
fits, not necessarily a simple word. Use jargon only with a reader who shares
it; explain any technical term you cannot avoid. When the text carries stiff,
official, or bureaucratic wording, read `references/words-to-avoid.md` and apply
the relevant swaps.

**Give instructions as commands.** "Remove the packet from the box", not "The
packet should be removed from the box" and not "You should remove the packet".
Imperatives are the most direct instruction. Add "please" to soften a request;
drop it when the action is not optional, since "please" invites refusal.

**Turn nominalizations back into verbs.** A nominalization is an abstract noun
made from a verb (discuss becomes discussion, provide becomes provision). Used
in place of its verb it makes the sentence sound like nothing is happening.
Write "We discussed the matter", not "We had a discussion about the matter".

**Use lists for parallel points.** Bullet points beat numbers or letters unless
order or reference matters, because they draw the eye without adding information
to absorb. Two forms:
- Separate points: full sentences take a capital and a full stop each; short
  fragments take neither, just consistent capitalization.
- One continuous sentence broken into points: end each point with a semicolon,
  put "and" or "or" after the second-to-last, start each point lower-case. Each
  point must read grammatically as a continuation of the introduction.

## Don't over-correct

These constructions are grammatical. Keep them where they read well; do not
stiffen a sentence just to obey a supposed rule against them:
- Starting a sentence with *and*, *but*, *because*, *so*, or *however*.
- Splitting an infinitive ("to boldly go").
- Ending a sentence with a preposition.
- Repeating a word when no better one fits.

## Apologising

When the text is a reply to a complaint or a difficult problem, read
`references/apologising.md` before writing.

## Done when

The points are in a logical order and every applicable rule has been checked:
sentences average 15 to 20 words and mostly carry one idea; verbs are active
unless a passive is deliberate; the reader is "you" and your side "we" where
that fits; words suit the reader and any jargon they would not know is cut or
explained; instructions read as commands; no nominalization stands in for its
verb; lists are grammatical and punctuated consistently. Then read the whole
once more to confirm it is clear, concise, helpful, human, and polite.
